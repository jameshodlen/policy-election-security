# Illinois

:material-check-circle:{ .tier-1 } **Tier 1** — High viability
{ .state-badge }

!!! abstract "At a glance"
    Illinois offers the strongest constitutional foundation in the nation for municipal polling place protection ordinances. The 1970 Constitution automatically grants home rule to every municipality over 25,000 residents, mandates liberal construction of home rule powers, and requires a three-fifths legislative supermajority to preempt local authority. Start here — Chicago first, then expand to the dozens of automatic home rule cities statewide.

!!! warning "Legal context"
    This analysis is for educational and advocacy purposes. It does not constitute legal advice. Consult legal counsel before taking action based on this information.

## The structural contradiction

Illinois Constitution Article III, Section 4 imposes a duty on the General Assembly to "insure the integrity of the election process."[^1] But unlike the state's bail-cost reimbursement mandate under Article I, Section 9, no equivalent funding mechanism exists for election security.[^1] The state requires local election integrity — then leaves municipalities to pay for it themselves.

Meanwhile, 18 U.S.C. § 592 makes it a federal crime to station "troops or armed men" at any polling place, punishable by up to five years' imprisonment and permanent disqualification from federal office.[^2] Yet this statute has seen near-zero federal enforcement.[^3] The gap between federal prohibition and federal action creates the opening for municipal civil enforcement.

## Legal framework

### Home rule authority

Illinois operates a dual system. Non-home-rule municipalities are governed by Dillon's Rule — they possess only powers expressly granted by the state.[^4] But the 1970 Constitutional Convention deliberately broke from that model for larger cities.

Article VII, Section 6(a) automatically vests home rule in any municipality with a population exceeding 25,000.[^1] No petition, no referendum, no state approval needed. These units may "exercise any power and perform any function pertaining to its government and affairs."[^1]

Two provisions make Illinois home rule unusually strong:

1. **Liberal construction mandate** — Article VII, Section 6(m) requires that home rule powers "shall be construed liberally."[^1] Courts must resolve ambiguity in favor of municipal authority, not against it. This effectively constitutionalizes the Cooley Doctrine — the principle that local self-government is an inherent right, not a state gift.[^4]

2. **Supermajority preemption barrier** — Under Article VII, Sections 6(g) and 6(h), the General Assembly needs a three-fifths majority to deny or limit any home rule power not currently exercised by the state.[^1] Home rule units may exercise powers concurrently with the state unless the legislature specifically declares state exercise to be exclusive.

### Preemption analysis

No existing Illinois statute expressly preempts municipal election protection ordinances. The state election code governs administration — voter registration, ballot design, canvassing procedures — but does not appear to occupy the field of polling place physical security in a way that would trigger implied preemption.

The three-fifths supermajority requirement creates an exceptionally high barrier. Even if a future legislature attempted to preempt local polling place protections, it would need 60% of both chambers — a threshold that has historically been difficult to achieve for contentious preemption efforts.

!!! tip "Bottom line"
    Illinois home rule cities can adopt polling place protection ordinances without waiting for Springfield. The legal authority is constitutional, the construction standard favors municipalities, and the preemption barrier is among the highest in the nation.

### Federal law interaction

Local ordinances must account for the federal statutory framework:

- **18 U.S.C. § 592** — Prohibits federal civil or military personnel from stationing troops or armed men at polling places.[^2] The sole exception: force necessary to "repel armed enemies of the United States."[^2]
- **18 U.S.C. § 1385 (Posse Comitatus Act)** — Prohibits use of the military to execute domestic laws. The 2021 NDAA expanded coverage to include the Navy, Marine Corps, and Space Force alongside the Army and Air Force.[^5]
- **10 U.S.C. § 252 (Insurrection Act)** — The primary exception allowing presidential military deployment for domestic enforcement.[^6]

??? note "Critical distinction: Title 32 vs. Title 10"
    National Guard troops under **state command (Title 32)** are not subject to the Posse Comitatus Act and may perform law enforcement functions at the governor's direction. Once **federalized (Title 10)**, they are treated as active-duty military and fall under full PCA and § 592 restrictions.[^5] Municipal ordinances must account for this distinction to avoid overreach.

## Political landscape

Illinois has unified Democratic control of the governorship and both legislative chambers. The state has a strong tradition of municipal self-governance, and Chicago in particular has a history of asserting local authority on civil rights and public safety matters.

The Illinois State Board of Elections holds "general supervision over the administration of the registration and election laws" under Article III, Section 5.[^1] Any ordinance campaign should engage the Board as a stakeholder for regulatory alignment — not because approval is legally required (it isn't, for home rule units), but because cooperation strengthens the political position.

## Recommended approach

### Sequencing

**Start with Chicago.** Three reasons:

1. **Maximum impact** — 2.7 million residents covered by a single ordinance
2. **Legal supremacy** — Article VII, Section 6(c) ensures that if Cook County adopts a conflicting measure, Chicago's ordinance prevails within city limits[^1]
3. **Political signal** — A Chicago ordinance creates momentum for the statewide campaign and national media attention

After Chicago, move to Cook County (covers unincorporated areas), then Aurora and other collar county cities to build a suburban coalition.

### Ordinance drafting strategy

Frame the ordinance under Article VII, Section 6(a) as a measure for the **"protection of the public health, safety, morals and welfare."**[^1] This exact phrasing triggers the Article VII, Section 6(m) liberal construction mandate — courts must construe the city's authority broadly.

The ordinance should include:

1. **Purpose statement** citing Article III, Section 3 ("all elections shall be free and equal") and Article XII, Section 2 ("military in strict subordination to civil power")[^1]
2. **Prohibition** mirroring 18 U.S.C. § 592's language on "troops or armed men" at polling places[^2]
3. **Civil enforcement mechanism** — injunctive relief and civil penalties, creating a local enforcement path independent of DOJ prosecution
4. **Suffrage protection** — explicit carve-out for Armed Forces members exercising their personal right to vote, per § 592[^2]
5. **Title 32/Title 10 distinction** — ordinance applies only to federalized (Title 10) military and federal civil service personnel, not state-commanded National Guard[^5]
6. **Insurrection Act harmonization** — align the ordinance's definitions with 18 U.S.C. § 594 (voter intimidation) to prevent characterization of local voter protection as obstruction of federal authority[^7]

### Key risks

!!! warning "Watch for"
    - **State preemption attempt** — Unlikely under current political conditions, but a future legislature could attempt to preempt with a three-fifths vote. Building a coalition of home rule cities creates political resistance to preemption.
    - **Federal preemption challenge** — 18 U.S.C. Chapter 29 § 104(a) provides that federal election provisions "supersede and preempt" state law for federal elections.[^8] Framing the ordinance as a health/safety/welfare measure rather than an "election regulation" avoids this trigger.
    - **Enforcement overreach** — An ordinance that attempts to restrict state-commanded National Guard (Title 32) would exceed municipal authority and invite legal challenge. Stick to federalized personnel only.

## What you can do

!!! tip "Action items"
    1. **If you're a Chicago resident**: Contact your alderperson and ask them to introduce a polling place protection ordinance citing 18 U.S.C. § 592 and the Illinois Constitution's free elections guarantee.
    2. **If you're in another Illinois home rule city**: Bring this analysis to your city council and request a resolution. Your city already has the legal authority — no state permission needed.
    3. **If you're an attorney**: Review the model ordinance framework above and help draft language for your jurisdiction. Pro bono election protection work is urgently needed.
    4. **If you're an organizer**: Connect with the coalition partners listed above. The campaign needs local champions in every target city.
